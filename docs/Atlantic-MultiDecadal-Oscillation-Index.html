1.2 Indicator name

Atlantic MultiDecadal Oscillation Index

Includes variable(s): ALL

1.3 Indicator brief description

The AMO is an observed pattern of alternating cool and warm periods of North Atlantic sea surface temperature every 40-60 years.

1.4 Indicator visualization

The indicator oscillates from 1850 to 1975 and then shows a generally increasing trend.

2.2.2 What implications of the indicators are currently listed?

The AMO was originally described as “an internal oscillation in the climate system resulting from interactions between North Atlantic ocean currents and wind patterns.” If it were an internal oscillation, it could theoretically be predicted and therefore potentially useful for forecasting. It is now thought to be “an artifact, during the historical era, of competing anthropogenic (greenhouse warming and sulphate aerosol cooling) drivers and, during the pre-historic era, an artifact of the fact that volcanic forcing happens to have displayed a roughly multidecadal pacing in past centuries.” See https://michaelmann.net/the-rise-and-fall-of-the-atlantic-multidecadal-oscillation/

2.2.3 Do target, limit, or threshold values already exist for the indicator?

No.

2.2.4 Have the indicators been tested to ensure they respond proportionally to a change in the underlying process?

Yes, the has been extensively tested using recent climate models to determine whether it is an internal climate system oscillation or not; the current conclusion is that it is not.
